Questions parents ask

Straight answers, before you sign up.

Is it really free?
Yes — completely free through our founding period, with no credit card required. We'll add affordable paid plans later, and founding families keep a locked-in early-bird price. You'll always see the price and agree to it before anything is ever charged.
Does the AI write my child's essays?
No. Our AI coach asks questions and helps your teen reflect — the writing stays in their own voice, and any AI assistance is disclosed transparently. Admissions offices now use AI to flag generic, template-written essays, so authentic writing built from real work is exactly what gets rewarded.
Do you guarantee admission, or fill out the application for us?
No — and be cautious of anyone who promises admission. We don't write or submit applications on a student's behalf. What we do is help your teen capture and organize four years of real work, so that when application season arrives, the story is specific, honest, and ready to tell.
What grade is the best time to start?
Eighth or ninth grade is ideal, because the whole point is to capture work as it happens instead of reconstructing it from memory. But any grade helps — even starting junior year beats scrambling the summer before deadlines.
Is our data private, and who owns it?
Your family's content is private to your account. You own what you upload, we never sell your data, and you can remove your material whenever you want.
We're not a Chinese-American family — is this for us?
Yes. TIPS is open to any student applying to US colleges. We built it first with the immigrant and Chinese-American families we know best, so the bilingual support and parent guides are unusually strong — but the four-year capture-and-reflect habit helps any applicant.
Does this replace our school counselor or private consultant?
No — it makes them more effective. TIPS is the capture-and-reflect layer underneath everything else. Bring your organized four-year record to whoever helps you apply, and their time goes to strategy instead of digging up what your teen did two years ago.
